# Ode to the Clause

O clause, subordinate and patient,
you who arrive only after *because*,
only after the cause has been sworn in —
how you carry the whole sentence on a *which*
and never ask to be the subject.

O dependent clause, you are not less.
You are the portion of the thought
that needs a host, the way a shadow
needs a leg to lean against.
Without you the main clause stands alone,
and standing alone is how sentences get arrested.

O relative clause, introduced by *who*,
you attach yourself like a barnacle of meaning
to the hull of a noun, and the noun
becomes a ship it never meant to be.

O clause, comma'd off, parenthetical,
you are the part of the poem
the poem is willing to lose
and yet, and yet — here you are,
trailing the sentence like a kite string
the sentence has forgotten it is holding.

> Epigraph: *The main clause gets the verb; the clause gets the grief.*
