# Sonnet With All Its Windows Broken

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Shall I compare thee to a shattered pane?
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Thou art more drafty and less weatherproof:
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;rough winds do shake the glass that's left, in vain,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;and summer's lease has voided through the roof.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Sometime too cold the air of winter blows,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;and often is the curtained room uncurtained;
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;and every pane from pane a nothing goes,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;when frame forgets the shape it once contained.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;But thy eternal glazing shall not stay,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;nor lose possession of the cold it owns,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;nor shall the landlord haul the glass away
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;so long as wind keeps singing through the bones;

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;so long as men can breathe, and birds fly in,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;this gives life to thee, and lets the weather win.

> Epigraph: *A sonnet is a window with fourteen panes. Some are already gone.*
