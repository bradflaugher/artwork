# unfollow

A triptych that turns the addictive social-media interface into fine-art refusal.
Three pieces, three traps made visible. Each is beautiful enough to stop the
scroll, then unsettling enough to make you want to close the app.

The work is drawn directly in vector code — SVG, committed as text — so the
medium is the same machinery the pieces accuse. The interface is the artifact;
the artifact is the indictment.

---

## Manifesto

We were promised connection. We were sold a slot machine.

The feed is not a window. It is a one-way mirror: you watch the spectacle,
the spectacle watches you, and every pause, every lingering glance, every
half-second of attention is metered, logged, and auctioned. The companies
that built these machines did not build them to inform you. They built them
to extract you — and to make the extraction feel like a favor.

This series refuses three of the lies, one per panel:

1. **The lie of abundance.** The infinite scroll presents itself as a feast.
   It is a void with a conveyor belt bolted on. There is no bottom because
   there was never anything at the bottom to reach. The feed does not end
   because ending would let you leave.
2. **The lie of care.** The red badge, the push notification, the gentle
   chime — these are staged as concern. *Someone thought of you. Someone
   needs you. Someone is waiting.* They are not waiting. A server is
   waiting, and the server was told to keep you here.
3. **The lie of presence.** The engagement counter converts your existence
   into a number and then, having done so, forgets you entirely the moment
   the number stops climbing. You were here. The metric proves it. The metric
   does not remember your name.

Pretty is fine. Pretty *and* dangerous is the point. These panels use the
visual grammar of the products they indict — the calm rounded corners, the
soothing palette, the frictionless gesture — and hold them still long enough
for you to notice what they are actually doing.

The words are part of the piece. A subversive image without context is just
an image. Read it as one work.

## Visual direction

All three pieces share a palette and a discipline:

- **Frame:** portrait, 9:16, the proportions of the phone that holds you.
- **Palette:** the warm-to-cold gradient of a day spent staring — dawn peach
  bleeding into surveillance blue, with a single recurring accent of
  notification red, `#FF3B30`, used only to mark the wound.
- **Typography:** the same soft, rounded, friendly sans-serif the apps use to
  seem harmless. The friendliness is the camouflage.
- **Discipline:** no human faces. The subject is the interface and the absence
  it manufactures. Where a person should be, there is a silhouette, a hole,
  a count.

The triptych moves from motion to pressure to absence:

| Panel | Gesture | Subversion |
|---|---|---|
| **the infinite scroll** | a feed that falls forever | vertigo where the product sells speed |
| **the notification abyss** | a red badge swelling into deep water | drowning where the product sells rescue |
| **the ghost engagement** | a like-counter draining to zero | haunting where the product sells belonging |

Read left to right: the trap, the alarm, the emptiness after.

## The pieces

### I. the infinite scroll

> *There is no bottom. There was never meant to be a bottom. The bottom
> would let you leave.*

A phone-shaped panel rendered in dawn-to-midnight blue. A vertical feed of
post-cards falls through it and out the bottom edge, the cards growing
smaller and more transparent as they recede — the promise of *just one more*
dissolving into nothing. A faint center axis marks the seam where the user's
thumb would be, the only fixed point in the descent. The notification-red
accent appears once: a single dot on one distant card, far down, where the
eye is trained to chase it.

The vertigo is the product. The speed is the extraction.

**File:** `the-infinite-scroll.svg` / `the-infinite-scroll.png`

### II. the notification abyss

> *It arrives as concern. It performs as need. It is a server, keeping you
> here.*

A dark teal panel, deep as water at dusk. At the top, the familiar rounded
red badge — `9`, then `99`, then `999` — swells as it descends, each digit
heavier, until the numbers lose meaning and the badge becomes a falling,
glowing weight sinking into the dark. Beneath it the silhouette of a head,
seen from above, tilts up toward the light of the badge the way a face tilts
toward a phone in a dark room. The red is the only color that reaches the
bottom. It reaches the bottom because it is meant to pull you down with it.

The alarm is manufactured. The urgency is the leash.

**File:** `notification-abyss.svg` / `notification-abyss.png`

### III. the ghost engagement

> *You were here. The number says so. The number does not remember you.*

A near-white panel, the color of a screen at 3 a.m. when the brightness has
been dropped so the room will stay dark. A single large engagement counter
sits at center: a heart, a number, a thin underline — the entire grammar of
*you matter, you are seen*. The number is frozen at a modest, very human
`247`. Around it, faint ghosted echoes of the same counter drift and fade,
each one a prior version of the self that the platform measured, used, and
discarded. No faces. No names. Just the residue of attention, already
cooling. The notification-red accent appears only as a single dying pixel in
the heart — the last trace of the machine that once cared, mechanically,
whether you existed.

The belonging was a number. The number has moved on.

**File:** `ghost-engagement.svg` / `ghost-engagement.png`

## On the medium

These are not photographs of an interface. They are the interface, redrawn by
hand in its own language and held still. SVG is text; text is inspectable;
inspectable is the opposite of the black box the feed prefers. Anyone can
open these files and read exactly how the trick was drawn — which is itself a
small refusal of the way these products usually work.

MIT-licensed. Take them apart. Repaint them. Show them in a place the feed
cannot reach.
